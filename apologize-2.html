The Small Loud Things

How teacups, calendars, and door hinges remember

The first thing that learned to speak after you left was the door. It sighed each time i opened it, as if rehearsing the sound of your shoes turning back. Then the calendar began to cough up dates with your initials—timid reminders scrawled in bubble letters "call back, bring peach jam, check plants". The basil obeyed you more than the sun, it drooped like a reprimand when i watered it too late.

I discovered that the house had a pulse. It tapped beneath the tiles at midnight, shuffled in the drawers where we hid our spare keys and our egos. Even the teacups chimed when i stacked them, a porcelain choir humming a chorus of almost-okay. None of it was dramatic. None of it was enough to drown out the quiet. But it was something—small loud things, refusing to be invisible.

If apologies are bridges, then i’ve been placing planks... a message drafted and unsent, a voice note deleted, a memory dusted and arranged on the mantle. I don't know if the river will cooperate. I only know the opposite of drowning is not always swimming. Sometimes it is naming the water.
